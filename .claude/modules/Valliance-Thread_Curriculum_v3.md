# The Valliance Thread

## A Continuous Learning Curriculum

---

## What This Is

The Valliance Thread is a permanent weekly institution that builds collective conviction across what Valliance believes, sells, builds, and delivers.

It is not a course with a start and end date. It is a continuous cycle that deepens with every rotation. Each cycle covers six arcs. Each arc runs for two weeks. After 12 weeks the cycle restarts, building on the positions already established. The Thread runs for as long as Valliance does.

It is not a reading club. It is not a show-and-tell. It is a structured process that forces the team to synthesise outside thinking against what Valliance sells and delivers, producing battle-tested position statements that the entire team can articulate cold.

Each session answers a question. The question is tied directly to an element of the PMF canvas or a Valliance capability. The answer must draw on external material and internal evidence, filtered through Valliance's lens. The output is written down every time.

---

## Structure

### The Arc

Six two-week arcs run sequentially. Each arc follows the same progression:

| Week | Type | Purpose |
|------|------|---------|
| 1 | **Problem + Landscape** | What is broken? How are others approaching it? Where do they fall short? Grounded in external and internal evidence. Includes mandatory Steelman. |
| 2 | **Position + Pitch** | What does Valliance believe and why? Deliver it under pressure. Includes mandatory Anti-Position, Cross-Arc Bridge, and Ghost Prospect. |

### The Cycle

One full cycle is 12 weeks. The Thread runs continuously.

| Weeks | Arc | Focus |
|-------|-----|-------|
| 1-2 | Arc 1: Agentic AI | Why enterprises fail at agentic deployment. What Valliance believes about building AI that works. |
| 3-4 | Arc 2: Palantir / Ontology | Why business logic is the hidden layer. What Valliance brings to ontology-led transformation. |
| 5-6 | Arc 3: People Enablement | Why adoption fails at the people layer. What Valliance believes about building capability, not compliance. |
| 7-8 | Arc 4: The Consulting Craft | Why the traditional consulting model is broken. What it means to be a Valliance consultant. |
| 9-10 | Arc 5: Agentic Engineering | How to build in the new world. The practitioner layer: architectures, tooling, evaluation, delivery. |
| 11-12 | Arc 6: Value Realisation | How to prove and sustain AI value. Making the outcome-based model work in practice. |

After Week 12, the cycle restarts at Arc 1. Each repetition goes deeper. The Week 1 question from Cycle 2 builds on the locked position from Cycle 1. Over 24 calendar weeks the team completes two full cycles, meaning two passes at every position rather than one.

### P1/V1 Lens

Valliance goes to market through two propositions: People First (P1) and Value First (V1). The Thread arcs are topic-based, not proposition-based. P1 and V1 are lenses applied within sessions:

- In Week 2 (Position + Pitch), the lead must explain how the position maps to P1, V1, or both
- The target persona in Pitch delivery should alternate between P1 and V1 buyer types across arcs
- Cross-Arc Bridges explicitly connect how arcs serve the P1 and V1 narratives

## The Friday Afternoon

The Thread runs every Friday afternoon in a 2.5-hour block. Three blocks, one break. Consistency removes friction and makes preparation predictable.

**Total duration:** 2 hours 30 minutes.

### Block 1: The Thread (45 min)

The core session. This is where the question gets answered and the position gets sharpened.

**Week 1 (Problem + Landscape) format:**

| Segment | Time | What Happens |
|---------|------|--------------|
| Live Fire | 5 min | Review real-world feedback on previously locked positions. If someone used an objection-response pair in a prospect meeting and it failed, that triggers a version update via the write-back process. If nobody has used the outputs, that silence is itself a signal. |
| Lead presents the Problem | 15 min | The lead answers the problem question, drawing on assigned reading, their own research, and the Evidence Vault. Must include at least one anonymised internal reference. |
| Lead presents the Landscape with Steelman | 15 min | The lead maps how others approach the problem, then spends 3 minutes arguing the strongest competitor case with genuine conviction. The Steelman is not a summary of a competitor's marketing page. It is the most compelling version of their argument, the one a smart buyer would find persuasive. |
| Team challenge | 5 min | The team pressure-tests the problem framing and landscape mapping. The goal is to find the holes. |
| Lock | 5 min | See "The Lock" below. |

**Week 2 (Position + Pitch) format:**

| Segment | Time | What Happens |
|---------|------|--------------|
| Live Fire | 5 min | Same as Week 1. |
| Lead presents Position | 15 min | The lead presents the draft position statement (reviewed by Shadow 24 hours prior), including the Anti-Position and Cross-Arc Bridge. The P1/V1 mapping is explicit. |
| Team sharpens | 15 min | The team co-edits the position: tightening the argument, testing the anti-position, checking the P1/V1 mapping, stress-testing the cross-arc bridge. |
| Lock the position | 10 min | See "The Lock" below. |

### The Lock (10 min, end of Block 1)

Every Thread session ends with a Lock. The purpose is to force the room to commit to a written output before anyone leaves Block 1. Without it, the debate stays verbal and evaporates over the weekend.

**How it works in Week 1 (Problem + Landscape):**

By this point in the session, the AI-augmented capture has already produced a draft structured summary of the problem framing and landscape discussion (see Capture Process below). The lead puts the draft on screen. The team reads it together and corrects it against what was actually said. The three things being locked are: (1) the problem statement (2-3 sentences defining what is broken and why), (2) the landscape criteria (the evaluation dimensions the Workshop will use to build the comparison grid), and (3) the Steelman summary (the strongest competitor argument, written down exactly as the lead articulated it, because the Position in Week 2 must directly address it). The lead marks the draft as "Locked" in Notion. It cannot be changed after this point except through the write-back process.

**How it works in Week 2 (Position + Pitch):**

The AI-augmented capture has produced a draft of the position statement incorporating the sharpening from the team discussion. The lead puts the draft on screen. The team reads it and corrects it. The four things being locked are: (1) the position statement itself (one page, what Valliance believes and why), (2) the Anti-Position (who we are the wrong choice for), (3) the Cross-Arc Bridge (how this position connects to previous arcs), and (4) the P1/V1 mapping (which proposition this serves). The lead marks it as "Locked" in Notion. This is the version that feeds the sales pipeline, the Forge storyboard, and the Fast-Track module. It does not change unless field evidence triggers a version update through Live Fire and the write-back process.

**What "Locked" means:** A locked output is the team's agreed position. It is not a draft. It is not "good enough for now." It is the statement the entire team is prepared to stand behind in a client conversation the following Monday. If someone is not comfortable with the locked version, they raise it in the 10-minute window. After the Lock, the only route to change it is through Live Fire (field evidence that it failed) or a Flash (market event that invalidates it). This constraint is deliberate. Without it, positions stay permanently provisional and nobody uses them with confidence.

### Break (15 min)

Non-negotiable. Concentration drops after 45 minutes of intense debate. The session recording starts processing during the break.

### Block 2: The Workshop (60 min)

The Workshop is where the Thread's output gets worked into something deeper. The format varies by week type:

**Week 1 (Problem + Landscape):** The team builds the Landscape Map collectively. The format runs as follows:

**Before Friday (preparation):** The lead assigns each team member one player or approach from the landscape to research. Assignments go out by Wednesday at the latest. If the arc is Agentic AI, one person takes Palantir AIP, another takes Anthropic's agent SDK, another takes the traditional SIs, another takes the "build it in-house" approach. Each person reads their assigned material (one piece from the reading list plus anything else they find) and prepares a 5-minute analysis. The analysis must answer three questions: (1) What is this player's core approach? (2) Where is it strongest? (3) Where does it break against the problems identified in the Thread?

**On the day (60 minutes):**

| Segment | Time | What Happens |
|---------|------|--------------|
| Set up the grid | 5 min | The lead puts the comparison grid on screen: players as columns, evaluation criteria as rows. The criteria come from the Problem discussion in Block 1 (e.g., for Agentic AI: governance capability, business logic handling, enterprise integration, autonomy-control balance). The team agrees the criteria before anyone presents. |
| Player presentations | 30 min | Each person presents their player (5 minutes each, assuming a team of 5-6). No slides required. The presenter talks through their three answers while the lead (or a nominated scribe) fills in the grid in real time. After each presentation, 1-2 minutes for quick clarifications only. Full debate comes later. |
| Cross-cutting debate | 15 min | The grid is now populated. The team steps back and looks at the whole picture. Where do multiple players fail on the same criterion? That is a structural gap in the market. Where does one player dominate? That is the position Valliance must either adopt, partner with, or explicitly counter. The team marks the grid: green for genuine strength, red for fundamental weakness, amber for "works in theory, fails in practice." |
| Problem-Landscape synthesis | 10 min | The team writes the one-paragraph summary that ties the problem (from Block 1) to the landscape (from the grid). The format is: "The market problem is X. The existing approaches each fail because Y. The gap that remains is Z." This paragraph becomes the opening of the Problem-Landscape Brief. The lead finalises the full brief by end of day Monday. |

The combined output replaces what would otherwise be two separate artefacts (a Problem Brief and a Landscape Map) with one integrated Problem-Landscape Brief anchored to a concrete comparison grid.

**Week 2 (Position + Pitch):** Multiple people deliver the pitch (not just the lead). Each delivery is 5-7 minutes. The Ghost Prospect and team provide feedback after each. By the end of the Workshop, 3-4 people have delivered the pitch and the group has seen which framings land and which do not. This distributes pitch capability across the team rather than concentrating it in one person. Objection-response pairs are forged live.

### Block 3: The Forge (30 min)

The Forge is where internal conviction gets shaped for the outside world. It is a storyboarding block, not a writing block. The assigned person leaves the Forge with three things: a clear outline of the external artefact, a list of Internal Evidence to include (drawn from the Evidence Vault), and one Ghost Prospect objection the piece must address head-on. The actual production happens during their Deep Work time the following week.

**The storyboard assignment:** Each Forge assigns one person to storyboard a specific external artefact. The artefact type rotates:

| Week Type | Forge Output |
|-----------|-------------|
| Problem + Landscape weeks | A data-backed "State of..." briefing or a comparison framework suitable for a blog post, LinkedIn article, or prospect conversation |
| Position + Pitch weeks | A Valliance viewpoint article, a credentials deck section, a sharpened pitch script, a client-facing one-pager, or a set of objection-response cards for the sales team |

**The Editor:** The Lead from the previous week acts as Editor for the current week's Forge output. They review the finished piece before it goes live, checking two things specifically: that the Anti-Position is correctly articulated (not softened or hedged) and that the Cross-Arc Bridge is present and accurate. The Editor is not a co-author. They are a quality gate. If the piece is not delivered by Thursday, the Editor escalates before Friday.

The Forge is not optional. The Thread only earns its 2.5-hour Friday slot if it produces external content at a sustainable cadence. After one full cycle, the Thread will have produced 12 external artefacts. Over two cycles (24 calendar weeks), that is 24 artefacts. That is the content pipeline.

### Capture Process (AI-Augmented)

We are an agentic AI consultancy. The capture process should reflect that.

Every session is recorded. The recording starts processing during the 15-minute break between Blocks 1 and 2. By the start of the Workshop, the team has a draft to work from. The transcript enrichment produces:

1. **A structured summary** of the key arguments made
2. **A disagreement log** highlighting where the team diverged (these are usually where the strongest positioning is hidden)
3. **A draft output** (Problem-Landscape Brief or Position Statement depending on the week type)

The "Lock" segment at the end of Block 1 is spent correcting and sharpening this draft rather than typing from scratch. The Workshop then builds on the locked output. The lead is responsible for finalising the output by end of day Monday.

### Week 1 Requirements

**The Steelman (mandatory, every Week 1):** The lead must spend 3 minutes of their Landscape slot arguing for the strongest competitor's position with genuine conviction. If the lead is mapping the agentic landscape, they must be able to argue the case for "just use Palantir's AIP directly" or "build it in-house with LangGraph" better than those players argue it themselves. If the lead cannot do this, the Position Statement in Week 2 will produce weak responses to strong objections.

A steelman is the opposite of a strawman. A strawman is a deliberately weakened version of someone's argument, built to be easy to knock down. A steelman is a deliberately strengthened version, built to be as hard to knock down as possible. The lead is not summarising a competitor's marketing page. They are constructing the most compelling version of the competitor's argument, the version a sharp buyer would find genuinely persuasive. Then the Team Challenge segment tears it apart. The purpose is defensive: if the Valliance position can only survive against a weak version of the competition, it will collapse the moment a prospect raises the strong version in a real conversation.

**Outside-In Evidence (mandatory, every Week 1):** The lead must bring at least one piece of external evidence from the Bookmarks DB that grounds the problem in something concrete: a case study, a data point, a named failure, a market report. The job is not to cite Valliance project history (which is thin at this stage). The job is to pull thinking from the outside world and filter it through the Valliance lens. The lead should explain not just what the evidence says, but what it means for Valliance specifically: does it validate our thesis, challenge it, or reveal a gap we have not addressed? As Valliance accumulates its own project evidence, those references layer on top of the external base rather than replacing it.

### Week 2 Requirements

**The Anti-Position (mandatory, every Week 2):** The lead must explicitly state what Valliance is the wrong choice for. The format is: "Because we believe X, we are the wrong choice for a client who wants Y." A position that tries to be everything to everyone is no position at all. The anti-position creates the repulsion logic needed for efficient GTM.

**The P1/V1 Mapping (mandatory, every Week 2):** The lead must explain how the position connects to the People First proposition, the Value First proposition, or both. Where does this position strengthen one proposition over the other? Does it create tension between them?

**The Cross-Arc Bridge (mandatory, every Week 2):** The lead must explain how the current arc's position reinforces, challenges, or changes positions from previous arcs. By Arc 6, the team should be able to articulate a unified Valliance thesis that connects all six arcs into a coherent narrative.

**The Ghost Prospect (mandatory, every Week 2):** An invited outsider (someone from outside the Thread, ideally from a different function) attends the Workshop and leads the objection round during pitch delivery. They lack curriculum context and will ask the questions a real buyer asks. Someone who has not been marinating in the arc's material for two weeks will expose assumptions the team has stopped noticing. Invite an engineer for a sales-oriented pitch, a recruiter for a technical pitch, or a designer for a strategy pitch. The more orthogonal their perspective, the better.

## Outputs

Each arc produces two categories of artefact: internal (from the Thread and Workshop) and external (from the Forge).

### Internal Artefacts (from the Thread and Workshop)

1. **Problem-Landscape Brief** (from Week 1): An integrated summary of the market or practice problem and how key players approach it, grounded in external and internal evidence. Includes the Landscape Map co-built in the Workshop.
2. **Position Statement with Anti-Position** (from Week 2): A one-page statement of what Valliance believes, why, how it translates to client value, who we are the wrong choice for, how it maps to P1/V1, and how it connects to previous arcs via the Cross-Arc Bridge.
3. **Objection-Response Pairs** (from Week 2): Battle-tested responses to the most common objections, forged in the Workshop under Ghost Prospect pressure.

### External Artefacts (from the Forge)

Each week produces one external artefact storyboarded in the Forge and produced during Deep Work the following week. Over one full cycle (12 weeks):

- 6 Problem-Landscape artefacts (briefings, comparison frameworks, market maps)
- 6 Position-Pitch artefacts (viewpoint articles, credentials deck sections, pitch scripts, objection-response cards)

That is 12 pieces of external content per cycle, 24 over two cycles. The Forge is the content pipeline.

### Accumulation

After one full cycle you have six position statements, a unified thesis, and 12 external artefacts. After two cycles you have twelve positions, each deeper than the last, and 24 external artefacts. The Thread produces the thinking. The Forge produces the output.

**Sales Feed:** Locked-in objection-response pairs from Week 2 sessions are pushed to the team's shared workspace (Slack channel or CRM) within 48 hours of the session via the write-back process. This is not optional.

## Process Guide

### Preparation Requirements

The Workshop block reduces solo prep burden. The team does much of the synthesis together on Friday rather than relying on one person to carry the full weight in advance.

**For the lead (Week 1: Problem + Landscape):**
- Read all assigned material (2-3 hours of reading and thinking across both problem and landscape)
- Prepare a 15-minute problem presentation and a 15-minute landscape presentation including the 3-minute Steelman
- Consult the Evidence Vault for internal references

**For the lead (Week 2: Position + Pitch):**
- Draft a one-page position statement before the session, including the Anti-Position, Cross-Arc Bridge, and P1/V1 Mapping
- Share with Shadow for review 24 hours in advance
- Prepare a 10-minute pitch delivery
- Consult the Evidence Vault for internal references

**For the shadow:**
- Read all assigned material (same as lead)
- Review the lead's draft (Position weeks) or presentation outline (Problem + Landscape weeks) 24 hours before the session and provide written feedback
- Be prepared to step in if the lead is pulled into a client emergency
- The shadow is not a co-presenter. They are a quality gate and a backup.

**For everyone else:**
- Read at least two of the assigned pieces (1 hour)
- Come with one challenge or question for the lead
- For Week 1: Be prepared to present one player or approach (5-7 minutes) in the Workshop
- For Week 2: Be prepared to deliver the pitch in the Workshop
- Bring any Live Fire feedback from real prospect or client conversations that tested a previously locked position

### Lead-Shadow Pairing

Every week has a Lead and a Shadow. The Shadow is assigned in the rota alongside the Lead.

The pairing serves two purposes: quality assurance (the Shadow sanity-checks the Lead's draft 24 hours before the session) and resiliency (in a high-growth consultancy, a Lead will eventually have a client emergency on their week; the Shadow can step in without the session being degraded).

The Shadow for Week N should ideally be the Lead for Week N+1 or the same arc's other week, so they are already immersed in the material.

### Rotation

The lead and shadow rotate each week. The rota is published for the full 12-week cycle at launch. Everyone knows their weeks in advance.

Suggested rotation principle: spread the Position + Pitch weeks (the high-stakes sessions) across different people. Pair less experienced team members as Shadow to a more experienced Lead early in the programme, then reverse the pairing in later arcs.

### The Bookmarks Pipeline

The Bookmarks database in Notion becomes the intake funnel for the Thread. When someone finds relevant material:

1. Add it to the Bookmarks DB
2. Tag it with the relevant arc (Agentic, Palantir/Ontology, People, Consulting, Engineering, Value)
3. Add a one-line note on why it matters

The lead for each week curates from the bookmarks rather than starting from scratch. The assigned reading in this curriculum is a starting point for Cycle 1; leads should supplement with newer material as it emerges. By Cycle 2, the bookmarks pipeline should be the primary reading source.

### The Flash Protocol

The Thread runs on a fixed 12-week cycle. The AI space does not. When something significant happens mid-cycle (a new model capability, a competitor pivot, a regulatory shift, a major client conversation that changes how we see the market), it cannot wait 10 weeks to be formally addressed.

**What qualifies as a Flash:** Not every interesting announcement is a Flash. The test is: "Does this change or invalidate a locked Valliance position?" If Anthropic ships a new agent protocol that makes the Arc 1 governance argument obsolete, that is a Flash. If a competitor publishes a blog post that says roughly what we already know, it is not.

**How it works:** Any team member can call a Flash by tagging it in the Evidence Vault with a `FLASH` label and naming the position it affects. The next Live Fire slot (5 minutes at the start of every session) expands to 10 minutes to accommodate the Flash review. The team decides one of three outcomes: (1) the existing position holds and the Flash is logged as context, (2) the position needs a minor version update (wording change, new objection-response pair), or (3) the position needs a major rewrite, which gets scheduled as a 15-minute Workshop insert the following week.

**Constraint:** No more than one Flash per session. If two Flashes land in the same week, the team triages by impact. This prevents the Flash from becoming a back door for "let's talk about whatever's trending" and derailing the current arc.

### Keeping Distant Arcs Alive

The Cross-Arc Bridge fires once per arc in Week 2, but a position locked in Week 2 could go untested for 10 weeks before the cycle restarts. The Flash Protocol handles acute disruptions. This mechanism handles gradual drift.

**The Arc Pulse (every 6 weeks):** Twice per cycle (at Weeks 6 and 12, aligning with Position + Pitch weeks for Arcs 3 and 6), the final 5 minutes of Live Fire is reserved for a rapid check on all six positions. The format: for each arc, does anyone have field evidence that challenges the locked position? If yes, it goes into the Evidence Vault tagged for review when the arc next comes around. If a position is under serious strain, it triggers a Flash.

**The Vault as passive refresh:** Every Live Fire update and Problem Week anecdote tagged in the Evidence Vault is visible to leads preparing for future arcs. A lead preparing for Arc 1 in Cycle 2 does not start from the Cycle 1 position alone. They start from the position plus every tagged piece of evidence that accumulated during the 10 weeks since it was locked. The Vault is the mechanism that keeps distant arcs current between formal sessions.

### Output Storage and Write-Back

All session outputs (Problem-Landscape Briefs, Position Statements, Pitch narratives, the Unified Thesis) are stored in a dedicated Notion space. Each arc gets its own section. Outputs are versioned so the deepening over successive cycles is visible.

**The write-back process:** When a position is updated (via Live Fire, Flash, or a new cycle's session), the following chain fires:

1. The lead who owns the update publishes the new version in the Output Storage space, incrementing the version number. The previous version is archived, never deleted.
2. The updated position is pushed to the sales feed (Slack channel, CRM, or both) within 48 hours, replacing the previous version. Anyone using the old objection-response pairs gets notified.
3. The Evidence Vault entry that triggered the update is linked to the new version, creating an audit trail from field evidence to position change.
4. The Fast-Track module is flagged for refresh. The module maintainer (rotating quarterly) updates it within one week. Until the update is live, new hires receive the previous version with a note flagging which positions are under revision.

This closes the loop. A position that fails in the field on Tuesday is updated in Live Fire on Friday, propagated to the sales feed by Sunday, and reflected in the Fast-Track within a week.

### The Evidence Vault

A dedicated space in Notion. Every time a Live Fire update is shared (a position that worked, a position that failed, a new objection encountered in the wild) or a Problem Week anecdote surfaces (an anonymised client story, a market data point, an internal build insight), it goes into the Vault. Each entry is tagged by Arc and Proposition (P1, V1, or both).

By Cycle 2, the Lead for any given week will not have to hunt for stories. They will have a searchable library of Valliance Truths to draw from: real evidence from real conversations, organised by topic and commercial lens. The Forge storyboards pull from the same Vault, ensuring that external content is grounded in first-hand evidence rather than borrowed thinking.

The Vault also feeds the Fast-Track. New hires reading the position statements alongside the evidence that shaped them will build conviction faster than reading positions in isolation.

### Thread Fast-Track (Onboarding)

By Cycle 2, the internal shorthand and logic will be deep enough that a new hire joining mid-cycle will feel alienated. The Fast-Track prevents this.

**Building the Fast-Track:** The AI-generated structured summaries and disagreement logs from each session are the raw material. After Cycle 1 completes, these are distilled into a 90-minute "Essential Thread" module covering: the six position statements, the six anti-positions, the unified Valliance thesis from Week 12, and the key disagreements that shaped each position.

**Using the Fast-Track:** Every new hire completes the module in their first two weeks. In their first month, they must "challenge" one of the existing position statements in a live Thread session. The challenge format: "I've read the position. Here's where I think it's wrong, and here's the evidence." This ensures new hires don't passively absorb tribal knowledge. They earn their seat by actively stress-testing what exists.

**Maintaining the Fast-Track:** After each cycle, the module is updated with the latest positions. Previous cycle positions become the "historical layer" that new hires can access but are not required to consume.

### What "Done" Looks Like

There is no "done." The Thread is continuous. But after one full cycle (12 weeks), every team member should be able to:

1. Articulate the market problem for each arc in under 2 minutes, grounded in evidence
2. Map the competitive landscape and explain where alternatives fall short
3. Deliver the Valliance position on each arc in a 10-minute pitch
4. Handle the three most common objections for each arc
5. Explain how all six arcs connect into the unified Valliance thesis
6. Articulate the P1/V1 proposition mapping for each position

This is testable. If someone cannot do it, the programme has not worked for them and needs to adapt.

### Measuring Success

Two metrics tell you whether the Thread is working.

**The Live Fire Metric:** Track the ratio of Positions Used vs. Positions Failed in the Live Fire slot. When an objection-response pair from Arc 2 gets used 10 times in sales conversations with a 90% success rate, that is a high-value asset. When a pair gets used 5 times and fails 4, that triggers an immediate version update via the write-back process. Over time, the Live Fire data reveals which positions are battle-hardened and which are still theoretical. If no positions are being used at all, the Thread is producing intellectual exercises rather than sales weapons.

**The Fast-Track Test:** New hires should be able to deliver the Unified Valliance Thesis (Week 12 output) by the end of their second week. If they cannot, the AI-augmented capture summaries are not clear enough, and the Fast-Track module needs rewriting. This is a canary for institutional knowledge quality. The new hire is not the problem. The summaries are.
